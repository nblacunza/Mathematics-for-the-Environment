Solving Nonlinear Equations

This chapter focuses on finding roots of equations where an exact algebraic solution is impossible—a fundamental challenge in practical applications.

"The characters of the universe are written in the language of mathematics." Explore how we translate these characters into Julia code.

Why Julia?

Numerical Methods Approach

In climate science, we very often need to determine where a function crosses zero—for example, when identifying equilibria or threshold behaviour. Understanding numerical root-finding methods is therefore essential.

Didactic Note: Every algorithm presented here balances three key factors: robustness (will it find the root?), speed (how many steps?), and requirements (do we need the derivative?).

Iterative Methods

An iterative method is a numerical technique used to approximate the solution of a mathematical problem through the repeated application of a well-defined update rule. Starting from an initial guess, the method generates a sequence of iterates that, under appropriate conditions, converges towards the desired solution.

Picard Iteration

Picard iteration is a fixed-point method that approximates the solution of a typically nonlinear equation by repeatedly evaluating a function, starting from an initial guess, until convergence to a fixed point is achieved.

Secant Method

The secant method is an iterative root-finding technique for typically nonlinear equations that updates the solution by intersecting a line through two successive approximations with the horizontal axis, avoiding the explicit computation of derivatives.

Explore Method →

Newton–Raphson

Fast convergence

The method updates the solution by linearising the function at each iterate using the tangent line (or the Jacobian in higher dimensions). Under suitable smoothness assumptions and for a sufficiently accurate initial guess, it exhibits quadratic convergence in the neighbourhood of a simple root.
